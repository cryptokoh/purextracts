How to Read a Certificate of Analysis (COA)

A beginner's guide to understanding lab test results and what to look for in quality botanical products. Learning to read COAs empowers you to make informed decisions.

What is a Certificate of Analysis?

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is a document issued by an accredited laboratory that shows the results of tests performed on a product. For botanical products like kratom, kava, and blue lotus, COAs verify purity, potency, and safety.

Think of a COA as a product's report card. It tells you exactly what's in the product and confirms it meets safety standards. Reputable vendors provide COAs for every batch they sell, and you should always review them before purchasing.

Key Sections of a COA

While COAs can vary in format between laboratories, most include these essential sections:

1. Laboratory Information

  • Lab Name and Address: The testing facility's identifying information
  • Accreditations: Look for ISO 17025 or similar certifications
  • Contact Information: Legitimate labs provide ways to verify results

2. Sample Information

  • Sample ID/Batch Number: Unique identifier for the tested batch
  • Sample Description: What product was tested
  • Date Received: When the lab received the sample
  • Date Tested: When testing was performed

3. Test Results

This is the most important section. For botanical products, look for:

Potency Testing

Shows the concentration of active compounds. For kratom, this includes mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine percentages. For kava, look for total kavalactone content. Results should be expressed as percentages or mg/g.

4. Contaminant Testing

This section confirms the product is free from harmful substances:

  • Heavy Metals: Lead, arsenic, mercury, cadmium (should all be below detection limits or within safe thresholds)
  • Microbial Testing: Bacteria counts, E. coli, Salmonella (should show "Not Detected" or "Pass")
  • Pesticides: Tests for common agricultural chemicals
  • Mycotoxins: Tests for mold-produced toxins

Red Flags to Watch For

Be cautious if a COA is missing contaminant testing, shows results that exceed safe limits, lacks a batch number, or can't be verified with the laboratory directly. Also be wary of COAs that are clearly altered or have inconsistent formatting.

Understanding Test Results

Here's how to interpret common result formats:

Pass/Fail Results

Some tests (especially microbial) show simple pass/fail results. "Pass" or "Compliant" means the product meets safety standards. "Fail" or "Non-Compliant" means it doesn't - avoid these products.

Numerical Results

Many results show specific numbers with units:

  • Percentage (%): Common for alkaloid content (e.g., "Mitragynine: 1.5%")
  • Parts per million (ppm) or ppb: Used for contaminants
  • mg/g: Milligrams per gram, another way to express concentration
  • CFU/g: Colony forming units per gram, used for microbial counts

Detection Limits

"ND" or "Not Detected" means the substance wasn't found at levels the test can measure. "< LOQ" means below the Limit of Quantification - present but too low to measure precisely. These results typically indicate a clean product.

Verifying a COA

Don't just trust that a COA is legitimate. Here's how to verify:

  1. Check the Lab: Google the laboratory name. Is it a real, accredited testing facility?
  2. Match Batch Numbers: Does the COA batch number match your product's label?
  3. Contact the Lab: Reputable labs can confirm they tested a specific sample
  4. Review the Date: Is the COA recent? Testing should be from the current batch
  5. Look for Completeness: Quality COAs include all relevant tests, not just potency

COA Quick Checklist

  • Lab is accredited (ISO 17025 or equivalent)
  • Batch number matches product label
  • Test date is recent (within 6-12 months)
  • Potency testing is included
  • Heavy metals testing shows safe levels
  • Microbial testing shows "Pass" or "Not Detected"
  • No signs of alteration or editing
  • Lab contact information is verifiable

Why COAs Matter

Requesting and reviewing COAs protects you in several ways:

  • Safety: Confirms products are free from harmful contaminants
  • Accuracy: Verifies you're getting what's advertised
  • Consistency: Helps you compare products and find reliable options
  • Accountability: Vendors who provide COAs stand behind their products

Conclusion

Learning to read COAs is one of the most valuable skills for any botanical consumer. It takes just a few minutes to review a COA, but that small investment of time can protect your health and ensure you're getting quality products.

Always ask for COAs before purchasing botanical products. If a vendor can't or won't provide current lab testing, consider that a red flag and look elsewhere.
